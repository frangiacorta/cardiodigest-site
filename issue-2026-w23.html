Industry & Regulatory

Short vs 12-month prasugrel + aspirin after ACS PCI with everolimus-eluting stents — Phase 4 trial

NCT06718179 (PHASE4) randomises patients with acute coronary syndromes treated with PCI and everolimus-eluting stents to a shortened versus 12-month course of prasugrel plus aspirin. The trial directly addresses the bleeding-ischaemia trade-off of dual antiplatelet duration in a contemporary potent-P2Y12 ACS population. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

CYP2C19 genotype-guided P2Y12 selection after complex PCI — Phase 4 trial

NCT06283888 (PHASE4) tests whether CYP2C19 genotype-guided selection of a P2Y12 inhibitor improves outcomes specifically after complex PCI, where ischaemic risk is high and loss-of-function alleles may blunt clopidogrel efficacy. Pharmacogenomic tailoring of antiplatelet therapy in complex anatomy remains an open question for the cath lab. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

Low-dose ticagrelor vs clopidogrel in stable elective PCI — Phase 4 trial

NCT06228456 (PHASE4) compares low-dose ticagrelor against clopidogrel in stable patients undergoing elective PCI, probing whether a reduced ticagrelor dose can preserve potency while limiting side effects in lower-risk elective cases. The comparison speaks to antiplatelet de-escalation strategies in stable coronary disease. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

PISTNT — perioperative intensive statin therapy for neuroprotection in TAVR — Phase 4 trial

NCT07087379 (PHASE4) evaluates whether perioperative intensive statin therapy provides neuroprotection in patients undergoing TAVR. With periprocedural cerebral embolisation an unresolved problem in structural intervention, a pharmacological neuroprotection strategy would be a novel adjunct. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

Antithrombotic strategy after mitral TEER without an anticoagulation indication — Phase 4 trial

NCT07007143 (PHASE4) is enrolling patients who undergo transcatheter edge-to-edge mitral repair and have no baseline indication for oral anticoagulation, comparing antithrombotic strategies for this common post-TEER scenario. No evidence-based standard currently exists for these patients, making the trial directly practice-informing for structural teams. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

Tier 1

Practice-changing

FLOW — Kidney and Survival Benefits of Semaglutide in Diabetic CKD by Cardiovascular Status
Tuttle KR et al. · J Am Coll Cardiol 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Cardiovascular disease increases the risks of CKD progression and mortality in type 2 diabetes. Whether the kidney and survival benefits of semaglutide in the FLOW trial are modified by baseline cardiovascular status was uncertain.
Methods
Prespecified subgroup analysis of FLOW, which randomised participants with type 2 diabetes and CKD to once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide 1.0 mg vs placebo. Baseline subgroups were atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD), heart failure, and — among those without established cardiovascular disease — high total cardiovascular risk (10-year PREVENT score ≥20%). The primary outcome was a composite of ≥50% eGFR decline, eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m², dialysis, transplantation, and kidney or cardiovascular death; all-cause death was a confirmatory secondary outcome. NCT03819153.
Key results
At baseline, 1,198 of 3,533 (33.9%) had ASCVD, 678 of 3,532 (19.2%) had heart failure, and 1,329 of 2,000 (66.5%) without established cardiovascular disease had high total risk. Semaglutide reduced the primary outcome consistently with and without ASCVD (HR 0.80; 95% CI 0.63–1.02; and HR 0.74; 95% CI 0.62–0.89; P for interaction=0.62), with and without heart failure (HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.49–0.93; and HR 0.79; 95% CI 0.67–0.93; P for interaction=0.40), and with and without high total risk (HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.58–0.91; and HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.49–1.08; P for interaction=0.99). Numbers needed to treat to prevent one primary kidney outcome at 3 years were 22, 13, and 17 in the ASCVD, heart-failure, and high-risk subgroups. Semaglutide also reduced all-cause death across subgroups.
Conclusion
Semaglutide improved kidney and survival outcomes in type 2 diabetes with CKD irrespective of established ASCVD, heart failure, or high total cardiovascular risk.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Prespecified subgroup analysis of a large, double-blind, placebo-controlled randomised trial. Consistency of effect across cardiovascular strata, with non-significant interaction tests throughout, strengthens the inference that benefit is not confined to a single phenotype.
Clinical relevance
The cath-lab population is increasingly defined by multimorbid cardio-kidney-metabolic disease. These data tell the interventionalist that a GLP-1 receptor agonist confers renal and mortality benefit in diabetic CKD whether or not the patient already carries an ASCVD or heart-failure label — and the lowest number needed to treat was in the heart-failure subgroup (13).
Context
FLOW established semaglutide's renal benefit in diabetic CKD; this analysis qualitatively extends that benefit across cardiovascular baseline strata. It aligns with the broader move to integrate SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, and finerenone into cardiorenal protection alongside revascularisation.
Limitations
Subgroup analysis with attendant reduced power within strata; the population is restricted to type 2 diabetes with CKD, so findings should not be extrapolated to non-diabetic CKD; some subgroup confidence intervals cross unity even where the overall effect is significant.
Bottom line: In diabetic CKD, semaglutide protects kidneys and improves survival regardless of whether the patient already has ASCVD, heart failure, or high total cardiovascular risk — reinforcing GLP-1 receptor agonism as a cardiorenal foundation in the multimorbid PCI patient.
INFINITY — Finerenone Across the Spectrum of Chronic Kidney Disease (Pooled IPD Analysis)
Neuen BL et al. · Lancet 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Overactivation of the mineralocorticoid receptor is a common pathway for CKD progression across aetiologies. Finerenone, a non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, has shown kidney and cardiovascular benefit in CKD due to type 2 diabetes, but its efficacy and safety across disease aetiologies and across levels of glycaemia, eGFR, and albuminuria had not been evaluated.
Methods
Individual participant data meta-analysis of three randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials of finerenone — FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD, and FIND-CKD. The main kidney outcome was kidney failure or a sustained ≥57% decline in eGFR; the main cardiovascular outcome was hospitalisation for heart failure or cardiovascular death. Cox regression was used for relative effects.
Key results
Across the three trials, 14,574 participants were included (mean age 63.7 years; 4,467 [30.7%] female; mean eGFR 56.4 mL/min/1.73 m²; median urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio 567.4 mg/g). Finerenone reduced the composite kidney outcome by 24% versus placebo (22.3 vs 28.8 events per 1,000 patient-years; HR 0.76; 95% CI 0.68–0.86) and kidney failure alone (HR 0.85; 95% CI 0.74–0.99). It reduced the composite cardiovascular outcome (19.1 vs 23.9 events per 1,000 patient-years; HR 0.80; 95% CI 0.70–0.91), including heart-failure hospitalisation (HR 0.78; 95% CI 0.66–0.92) and cardiovascular death (HR 0.82; 95% CI 0.67–0.999), and reduced all-cause death (HR 0.88; 95% CI 0.79–0.99). Effects on the kidney outcome were consistent irrespective of glycaemic status, CKD aetiology, baseline eGFR, albuminuria, and SGLT2-inhibitor use. Hyperkalaemia was more frequent with finerenone, but hospitalisation for hyperkalaemia was uncommon.
Conclusion
Finerenone reduced CKD progression, heart-failure hospitalisation, cardiovascular death, and all-cause death across a broad range of CKD aetiologies and disease severities.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Individual participant data meta-analysis pooling three placebo-controlled randomised trials — the most rigorous synthesis design — with the addition of FIND-CKD extending the evidence beyond diabetic kidney disease. Central kidney and cardiovascular endpoints were prespecified and harmonised.
Clinical relevance
The all-cause mortality reduction (HR 0.88) and the heart-failure-hospitalisation benefit position finerenone as a cardiorenal agent, not merely a nephrology drug. Consistency of the kidney effect across glycaemic status and aetiology supports use in non-diabetic CKD, broadening the candidate pool that the interventional cardiologist co-manages.
Context
The three constituent trials anchor the modern mineralocorticoid-receptor-antagonist evidence base; this pooled analysis is the formal cross-aetiology synthesis that complements the cardio-kidney-metabolic management paradigm and sits alongside SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 agonists.
Limitations
Pooling trials with differing inclusion criteria and follow-up; hyperkalaemia remains the principal safety trade-off and demands potassium monitoring; the cardiovascular-death confidence interval reaches close to unity (upper bound 0.999); aetiology subgroups beyond diabetes, while consistent, are smaller.
Bottom line: Pooled across 14,574 patients, finerenone cuts CKD progression by roughly a quarter and lowers heart-failure hospitalisation, cardiovascular death, and all-cause death across CKD aetiologies — consolidating its place as a foundational cardiorenal therapy, with hyperkalaemia the manageable cost.
TRACK — Low-Dose Rivaroxaban and Cardiovascular Events in Advanced Kidney Disease
Badve SV et al. · JAMA 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Roughly 10%–15% of patients with advanced CKD experience a fatal or nonfatal cardiovascular event each year, yet the effects of antithrombotic therapy on cardiovascular events in advanced CKD are unknown.
Methods
Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial at 90 centres in 12 countries. Eligible adults had CKD stage 4 or 5 or dialysis-dependent kidney failure, plus a history of coronary artery disease, nonhemorrhagic nonlacunar stroke, peripheral artery disease, diabetes, or age ≥65 years. Patients were randomised 1:1 to rivaroxaban 2.5 mg twice daily or placebo. The primary outcome was a composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal MI, stroke, or a peripheral artery disease event; the primary safety outcome was major bleeding. NCT03969953.
Key results
Of 1,458 randomised patients (mean age 63.2 years; 432 [29.6%] female), 1,360 (93.3%) completed follow-up; the trial was stopped early for lack of efficacy. Over a median 1.7 years, the primary outcome occurred in 164 patients (22.6%) on rivaroxaban and 151 (20.7%) on placebo (13.0 vs 11.8 events per 100 person-years; HR 1.09; 95% CI 0.87–1.36; P=0.46). Major bleeding occurred in 64 (8.8%) vs 44 (6.0%) (5.1 vs 3.4 events per 100 person-years; HR 1.51; 95% CI 1.02–2.22; P=0.04).
Conclusion
In advanced CKD at high cardiovascular risk, low-dose rivaroxaban did not reduce cardiovascular events and significantly increased major bleeding.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Multinational, double-blind, placebo-controlled randomised trial — the appropriate design to answer a long-standing evidence gap — terminated early for futility, with high follow-up completeness (93.3%).
Clinical relevance
Advanced-CKD patients are systematically excluded from most antithrombotic trials, and the temptation to extrapolate low-dose rivaroxaban (effective in COMPASS-type populations) to this group is strong. TRACK closes that door: no ischaemic benefit and a 51% relative increase in major bleeding. For interventionalists, it argues against routine low-dose anticoagulant add-on for secondary prevention in stage 4–5 CKD.
Clinical context
Patients with advanced CKD carry both high atherothrombotic and high bleeding risk, and prior antithrombotic strategies have repeatedly foundered on that balance; this is the first dedicated randomised cardiovascular-outcomes test of low-dose rivaroxaban in the population.
Limitations
Early termination reduces power and precision; the broad enrolment criterion (including age ≥65 or diabetes alone) yields a heterogeneous high-risk cohort; median follow-up was short at 1.7 years; the result is a null/harm signal rather than a tested alternative regimen.
Bottom line: Low-dose rivaroxaban should not be used for cardiovascular prevention in advanced CKD — TRACK shows no ischaemic benefit and a significant excess of major bleeding, and was stopped early for futility.
Tier 1 FINE-HEART — Finerenone and Sudden Death Across the Cardio-Kidney-Metabolic Spectrum
Foà A et al. · J Am Coll Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

This prespecified FINE-HEART pooled analysis combined participant-level data from three placebo-controlled finerenone trials (FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD, FINEARTS-HF) totalling 18,991 patients to examine predictors of and treatment effects on adjudicated sudden death. Over a median 2.9 years, 418 participants (2.2%; 0.77 per 100 patient-years) had sudden death, and randomisation to finerenone reduced sudden death versus placebo (HR 0.81; 95% CI 0.67–0.98; P=0.034), with consistent relative reductions across number of baseline cardio-kidney-metabolic conditions and across trials. The finding adds an antiarrhythmic-mortality dimension to finerenone's established cardiorenal profile, though sudden death was a pooled secondary endpoint and mechanisms remain to be defined.

Tier 1 ALIGN — Atrasentan in IgA Nephropathy: Final 2.5-Year Results
Heerspink HJL et al. · Lancet 2026 · source ↗

In this phase 3, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial across 133 sites in 20 countries, 404 adults with biopsy-proven IgA nephropathy on renin-angiotensin blockade were randomised to atrasentan 0.75 mg daily or placebo for 132 weeks. In the main stratum the change in eGFR at week 136 was −7.5 mL/min/1.73 m² with atrasentan versus −9.9 with placebo (between-group difference 2.4; 95% CI −0.1 to 4.8; P=0.057), with a total eGFR slope difference of 1.4 mL/min/1.73 m²/year (95% CI 0.5–2.3) favouring atrasentan and fluid-retention events balanced between arms (14% vs 12%). The selective endothelin A receptor antagonist preserved kidney function with or without concomitant SGLT2 inhibition, though the headline eGFR endpoint narrowly missed conventional significance.

Tier 1 SMuRF — Controlled Modifiable Risk Factors Before PCI and One-Year Outcomes
Nicolas J et al. · Eur Heart J 2026 · source ↗

In 19,651 patients undergoing PCI at a single tertiary centre (2012–2023), pre-procedural control of four standard modifiable risk factors — blood pressure, glucose/HbA1c, LDL, and smoking — was assessed and patients stratified by number of controlled factors. Compared with full control of all four, the one-year risk of MACE (death, MI, or stroke) rose progressively as control was lost (2 controlled: adjusted HR 1.39, 95% CI 1.14–1.69; ≤1 controlled: adjusted HR 1.48, 95% CI 1.10–2.00), and glycaemic control showed the strongest single association with lower MACE (adjusted HR 0.56, 95% CI 0.49–0.65). Comprehensive risk-factor control before PCI was infrequent yet strongly associated with better prognosis, with glucose control offering the greatest individual gain.

Tier 2

Notable

— IVUS-Guided Planned Rotational Atherectomy vs Cutting Balloon for Grade III Calcified Lesions
Yu H et al. · Am J Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

In this single-centre prospective study, 80 patients with grade III coronary calcified lesions were randomised to IVUS-guided planned rotational atherectomy (RA) or cutting balloon (CB), 40 per arm, with strategy success and stent expansion as the primary endpoints. Planned RA achieved higher strategy success (95% vs 80%; P=0.043) and significantly better stent-expansion indices, at the cost of higher X-ray dose, contrast, and procedural expense, with no difference in 30-day MACE. IVUS-guided planned RA outperformed cutting balloon for severe calcium pretreatment, though the small single-centre sample warrants confirmation before routine adoption.

— Cerebral Embolic Protection During TAVR: Updated RCT-Only Meta-Analysis
Sayed MS et al. · Front Cardiovasc Med 2026 · source ↗

This PRISMA-guided meta-analysis restricted to randomised trials pooled nine RCTs comprising 11,696 TAVR patients (6,000 with cerebral embolic protection, 5,696 control) to test whether protection devices reduce stroke. Cerebral embolic protection did not significantly reduce all-cause stroke (RR 0.92; 95% CI 0.73–1.14; P=0.43), with consistent null results for Sentinel and TriGuard and no benefit for mortality, disabling stroke, or major vascular complications. The randomised evidence continues to argue against routine cerebral protection in unselected TAVR, leaving any role to be defined in higher-risk subsets.

— Self-Expanding vs Balloon-Expandable TAVR in Small and Extra-Small Annuli
Abdelrahman AK et al. · Cardiol Rev 2026 · source ↗

This systematic review and meta-analysis identified 25 studies encompassing 13,846 patients (5,633 balloon-expandable; 8,213 self-expanding) with CT-defined small aortic annuli. Self-expanding valves delivered superior haemodynamics — larger indexed effective orifice area (MD 0.20 cm²/m²), lower mean gradient (MD −4.11 mmHg), and less severe patient-prosthesis mismatch (OR 0.37) — but higher permanent pacemaker (OR 1.63) and moderate/severe paravalvular leak (OR 2.26), with no difference in 1-year mortality or 30-day stroke. Notably, in the extra-small annulus subgroup the haemodynamic advantage persisted while the pacemaker and leak penalties were no longer statistically significant, supporting anatomy-individualised valve selection.

EMPIRE I — ALLEGRA Valve With Repositionable IMPERIA Delivery System: 1-Year Results
Baz JA et al. · Catheter Cardiovasc Interv 2026 · source ↗

EMPIRE I was a pre-market, prospective, multicentre, single-arm study at 11 European centres enrolling 121 patients with severe native aortic stenosis and 16 with degenerated surgical bioprostheses treated with the ALLEGRA valve via the fully repositionable IMPERIA delivery system, adjudicated with VARC-3 definitions and an independent echo core lab. At 1 year, bioprosthetic valve dysfunction was 5.9% in the native cohort with no structural valve deterioration or bioprosthetic valve failure, new pacemaker implantation was 14.3% at 30 days (unchanged at 1 year), and all-cause mortality was 10.8% (95% CI 6.4%–17.8%) in the native population and 0% in valve-in-valve patients. The repositionable system showed durable haemodynamic performance, though the single-arm design and modest sample preclude comparative claims.

— Trajectories of Cardiac Damage Staging After TAVR and Long-Term Outcomes
Yoshida T et al. · Cardiovasc Interv Ther 2026 · source ↗

In a single-centre registry of 762 TAVR patients with echocardiography at baseline, immediately post-procedure, and 1 year, cardiac damage staging (CDS) was tracked and dichotomised as early (stage 0–2) versus advanced (stage 3–4). Among baseline advanced-stage patients, 58.1% improved to early stage by 1 year, whereas 7.1% of baseline early-stage patients worsened; advanced CDS at 1 year was associated with higher all-cause mortality (adjusted HR 1.90; 95% CI 1.10–3.29; P=0.02), and absence of atrial fibrillation predicted improvement while AF and mild tricuspid regurgitation predicted worsening. CDS is dynamic in the first year after TAVR and the 1-year stage — not just the baseline stage — carries prognostic weight.

— Phenotyping Low-Flow, Low-Gradient Aortic Stenosis With Preserved EF Undergoing TAVI
van Bergeijk KH et al. · ESC Heart Fail 2026 · source ↗

Among 827 TAVI patients at a Dutch tertiary centre, 216 (35%) had paradoxical low-flow, low-gradient aortic stenosis with preserved ejection fraction, and unsupervised clustering of AI-assessed echocardiographic parameters separated them into two phenotypes. Cluster 1 (n=77) carried a distinct HFpEF signature — larger atrial dimensions, impaired global longitudinal strain, more atrial fibrillation, higher HFpEF scores — and had higher 1- and 5-year cardiovascular mortality and heart-failure hospitalisation than cluster 2, with internal and external validation. One-third of these patients have a high-risk HFpEF/AF phenotype that may warrant guideline-directed HFpEF therapy alongside valve replacement.

— Invasive Transvalvular Flow Rate to Identify Likely Severe Low-Gradient Aortic Stenosis
Elbasha K et al. · Clin Res Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

In a single-centre registry of 134 patients with low-gradient severe aortic stenosis and normal ejection fraction undergoing pre-TAVI invasive haemodynamics, the transvalvular flow rate (TFR) was compared between ESC-defined likely- and unlikely-severe disease. The likely-severe group had a lower TFR (159 vs 180 mL/s; P=0.044), a TFR ≤200 mL/s was strongly associated with likely-severe disease (adjusted OR 3.09; 95% CI 1.30–7.34), and integrating TFR predicted 5-year cardiac mortality (adjusted HR 0.45; 95% CI 0.21–0.97) where stroke volume index did not. Invasive TFR may refine the diagnosis of discordant low-gradient aortic stenosis and carry prognostic value beyond conventional flow markers.

— TEER vs Transcatheter Replacement vs Medical Therapy in Tricuspid Regurgitation: Network Meta-Analysis
Veettil INK et al. · J Cardiothorac Surg 2026 · source ↗

This network meta-analysis of three randomised trials (1,050 patients, with optimal medical therapy as common comparator) compared tricuspid TEER and transcatheter tricuspid valve replacement (TTVR) against medical therapy for moderate-to-severe tricuspid regurgitation. Neither TEER (RR 0.99; 95% CI 0.56–1.76) nor TTVR (RR 0.85; 95% CI 0.51–1.41) reduced all-cause mortality, but both improved NYHA class and KCCQ quality of life beyond the minimal clinically important difference, with TTVR ranked best for functional endpoints (Bayesian posterior probability 100% for NYHA) at the cost of more pacemaker implantation (number needed to harm 10). Both transcatheter options reliably improve symptoms and quality of life without a demonstrated survival benefit, and head-to-head trials are still needed.

— Single-Session Pulsed-Field Ablation Plus Mitral TEER: Ten-Patient Prospective Series
Konecna AC et al. · Struct Heart 2026 · source ↗

This research correspondence reports the first expanded cohort combining pulsed-field ablation (PFA) with mitral transcatheter edge-to-edge repair via a single transseptal access in ten high-risk patients. Sinus rhythm was maintained in 90% at 6 months with significant NYHA improvement (P=0.008), no strokes, tamponade, or PFA-related complications, and a median procedure time of 102 minutes. PFA tissue selectivity appears to enable a feasible one-stop approach to concomitant atrial fibrillation and mitral regurgitation, though the ten-patient series is hypothesis-generating only.

DEFINE-HT — Donor-Derived Cell-Free DNA as a Prognostic Biomarker After Heart Transplant
Shah P et al. · JACC Heart Fail 2026 · source ↗

DEFINE-HT was a prospective observational study following 110 adult heart-transplant recipients at 10 US centres for one year, with donor-derived cell-free DNA (dd-cfDNA) sampled independent of clinical decisions. The primary composite of treated rejection, graft dysfunction, retransplantation, or death occurred in 38 patients (34.5%), and each one-intrapatient-SD rise in dd-cfDNA% or donor quantity score was associated with 19% and 12% higher event risk (P<0.001), with biomarker levels higher in treated than untreated biopsy-positive rejection. dd-cfDNA carries prognostic signal beyond histology, though notably no patient with graft dysfunction had biopsy-positive rejection despite elevated biomarker — underscoring its complementary rather than substitutive role.

J-PVAD — Impella Timing Relative to PCI in AMI Cardiogenic Shock on VA-ECMO
Homma T et al. · Heart Lung 2026 · source ↗

From the Japanese Registry for Percutaneous Ventricular Assist Device, 402 patients with acute MI cardiogenic shock treated with ECPELLA (VA-ECMO plus Impella) and PCI were classified by Impella timing (pre-PCI n=303 vs post-PCI n=99), with 87% having cardiopulmonary arrest before Impella placement. All-cause 30-day mortality did not differ by timing (adjusted HR 1.000; 95% CI 0.826–1.211; P=0.997), and within the pre-PCI group a signal of higher risk with door-to-unload >90 minutes (HR 1.386; 95% CI 0.99–1.94; P=0.057) was inconclusive. Impella timing relative to PCI was not associated with survival in this severely ill registry cohort, leaving optimal unloading strategy to randomised trials.

Tier 3

Worth knowing

— Statin Use and Intensity and Renal Outcomes After TAVR
Abu Khadija H et al. · Cardiorenal Med 2026 · source ↗

In a retrospective cohort of 810 consecutive TAVR patients (620 statin users, 190 non-users), preprocedural statin use and intensity were related to 30-day renal outcomes. Statin use increased the likelihood of GFR improvement (average marginal effect 20.7%; 95% CI 12.3%–29.1%) and lowered acute kidney injury (adjusted HR 0.525; 95% CI 0.313–0.881), whereas high-intensity statins raised the risk of GFR deterioration and AKI (adjusted HR 2.85; 95% CI 1.52–5.34). The data suggest statin therapy is renoprotective around TAVR but that high intensity may carry early renal risk, arguing for individualised peri-procedural dosing pending randomised confirmation.

— Fast-Track Next-Day Discharge After TAVI: Feasibility and Safety
Rahmani S et al. · Arch Cardiovasc Dis 2026 · source ↗

This single high-volume-centre retrospective study of 479 TAVI patients stratified them into fast-track-ineligible, next-day-discharge-achieved, and early-discharge-failed groups. Fast-track eligibility was met in 80.8% (95% CI 77.2%–84.0%), next-day discharge was achieved in 69.9% of eligible patients, and conduction disturbances were the leading cause of failed early discharge (55.9%), with 3-month cardiovascular events of 4.4% in the next-day group versus 11.4% in ineligible patients (P=0.06). Next-day discharge after TAVI was feasible and safe in selected patients, with conduction disturbance the principal obstacle to wider adoption.

BATAC — Balloon-Assisted Transaxillary Artery Closure in Percutaneous TAx-TAVI
Apostolos A et al. · Am J Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

This observational series described a balloon-assisted transaxillary artery closure (BATAC) technique in 12 patients undergoing fully percutaneous transaxillary TAVI under local anaesthesia, using two pre-deployed suture devices with adjunctive balloon inflation during closure. Technical success was achieved in all patients and device success in 91.7%, with no in-hospital deaths, cerebrovascular events, or major vascular complications, and one tamponade managed by drainage. The simplified percutaneous axillary closure strategy showed promising early outcomes, but the 12-patient cohort requires larger validation.

— TAVR vs SAVR in Patients With a Pre-Existing Pacemaker or ICD
Abomohsen M et al. · Cardiol Rev 2026 · source ↗

Using the National Inpatient Sample (2016–2022), this analysis compared isolated TAVR with SAVR in patients with aortic stenosis and a pre-existing permanent pacemaker or ICD, forming 501 propensity-matched pairs from 8,776 hospitalisations. In-hospital mortality was lower with TAVR (0.80% vs 2.59%; matched OR 0.31; 95% CI 0.10–0.94; P=0.049), with lower major adverse cardiovascular and cerebrovascular events, stroke/TIA, AKI, and shorter length of stay and no excess new-pacemaker procedures. Where the usual pacemaker disadvantage of TAVR is already neutralised by a pre-existing device, TAVR appears favourable over surgery in this subgroup.

— CT-Defined Myosteatosis Predicts MACE and Mortality After TAVR
Li Z et al. · J Nutr Health Aging 2026 · source ↗

In 371 TAVR patients (198 men, 173 women), CT-defined myosteatosis and low muscle mass measured at the T12 level were related to outcomes, with 71 patients (19.1%) experiencing MACE and 46 (12.4%) dying. Myosteatosis was associated with higher MACE (HR 2.39; 95% CI 1.13–5.05) and all-cause mortality (HR 3.83; 95% CI 1.54–9.53), and patients with both myosteatosis and low muscle mass had the worst outcomes (MACE HR 5.06; 95% CI 2.26–11.32). CT-derived body composition adds modest prognostic information after TAVR, though external validation is needed before clinical use.

CALLY — CRP-Albumin-Lymphocyte Index as a Prognostic Marker in TAVI
Çelik A et al. · J Cardiothorac Surg 2026 · source ↗

In a single-centre retrospective cohort of 330 patients undergoing TAVI for severe aortic stenosis, the composite CRP-albumin-lymphocyte (CALLY) index was evaluated against all-cause mortality. The index was lower in patients who died than in survivors (1.0 vs 3.0; P<0.001), independently predicted mortality (HR 0.965; 95% CI 0.933–0.997), and a cut-off of 2.21 discriminated mortality with an AUC of 0.730. This simple, inexpensive inflammatory-nutritional biomarker may aid early risk stratification before TAVI, pending prospective validation.

J-PRIDE — FFR-Guided Revascularisation Using Nicorandil vs Conventional Hyperaemic Agents
Nogi K et al. · Cardiovasc Interv Ther 2026 · source ↗

From the J-PRIDE registry, 2,369 patients (3,295 lesions) assessed with nicorandil were compared with 831 patients (1,009 lesions) assessed with adenosine or papaverine for the 1-year incidence of target-vessel failure after FFR-guided decisions. Target-vessel failure did not differ between nicorandil and non-nicorandil groups (3.2% vs 2.4%; adjusted HR 1.23; 95% CI 0.78–1.94; P=0.37), with comparable rates in both deferred and revascularised populations and a significant interaction only with haemodialysis. Outcomes following nicorandil-based FFR were comparable to conventional agents, supporting its clinical applicability for hyperaemia induction.

— High-Sensitivity Troponin T to Identify Obstructive CAD in NSTE-ACS
Khokhar A et al. · Cardiovasc Revasc Med 2026 · source ↗

This study evaluated the diagnostic yield of high-sensitivity cardiac troponin T for identifying obstructive coronary artery disease in patients presenting with non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndrome. The analysis characterises how troponin thresholds relate to angiographically obstructive disease in this population, informing pre-angiography risk assessment. As a diagnostic-accuracy study, it adds to the evidence on biomarker-guided triage in NSTE-ACS rather than to revascularisation strategy.

— Reduced Cu,Zn-Superoxide Dismutase and Coronary Microvascular Dysfunction in ANOCA
Sato K et al. · Int J Cardiol Heart Vasc 2026 · source ↗

In this nationwide-registry study of 295 ANOCA patients undergoing coronary flow reserve and index of microcirculatory resistance measurement, serum oxidative-stress and endothelial markers were related to coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD). CMD was present in 43% of patients and was independently predicted by Cu,Zn-superoxide dismutase below 97.2 ng/mL (OR 2.71; 95% CI 1.56–4.74; P<0.001), with Cav-1 particularly reduced when both CFR and IMR were abnormal. Reduced antioxidant capacity is associated with CMD, supporting systemic oxidative stress as a contributing mechanism in ANOCA.

— Pulmonary Hypertension and Long-Term Outcomes After PCI for ACS
Awad A et al. · Heart Lung 2026 · source ↗

In a single-centre registry of 3,022 ACS patients undergoing PCI (2012–2017), 158 (5.23%) had pre-existing pulmonary hypertension, which was related to in-hospital and post-discharge outcomes after multivariable adjustment. Pulmonary hypertension independently predicted cardiogenic shock (adjusted OR 3.32; 95% CI 1.59–6.95), AKI (adjusted OR 1.72), and in-hospital heart failure (adjusted OR 2.15), with higher 6-month heart-failure readmission and stroke but similar mortality and recurrent ACS. Pulmonary hypertension flags a congestion- and haemodynamics-driven high-risk PCI phenotype rather than an atherothrombotic one.

— Female Sex as a Risk Modifier Rather Than Risk Factor in Atrial Fibrillation
McGarvey C et al. · JACC Adv 2026 · source ↗

Using the TriNetX network, this propensity-matched analysis examined whether female sex is an independent stroke risk factor or a risk modifier in nonvalvular atrial fibrillation, stratified by age. Among patients without additional CHA₂DS₂-VA risk factors (n=252,528), female sex raised stroke risk only at age ≥75 years (HR 1.244; 95% CI 1.087–1.423), and a similar pattern held with one additional risk factor (HR 1.065 at ≥75 years). Female sex behaves as a modest risk modifier whose effect concentrates in older, higher-comorbidity patients, consistent with the sex-as-modifier reframing in recent risk schemes.

STRONG-DM — NT-proBNP-Guided Heart-Failure Prevention in Diabetes: Pilot Study
Subramanian V et al. · JACC Heart Fail 2026 · source ↗

This pilot study tested a virtual cardio-kidney-metabolic prevention strategy in patients with type 2 diabetes and no heart failure, using primary-care education, EHR-prompted NT-proBNP screening, and virtual consultations against matched controls. NT-proBNP screening was ordered in 93.4% of patients, identified 16.7% additional high-risk patients beyond the WATCH-DM score, and the intervention group had significantly more new prescriptions of SGLT2 inhibitors (20.3% vs 5.8%; P<0.001) and finerenone (4.1% vs 0.2%). A biomarker-guided, technology-enabled prevention pathway increased uptake of guideline cardiorenal therapies in diabetes, supporting a larger trial.

Landmark Revisited

Landmark Trial
IABP-SHOCK II
Thiele H et al. · N Engl J Med 2012 · PMID 22920912 · source ↗
Why revisit now?

Active LV unloading added to VA-ECMO is the focus of the current generation of mechanistic randomised trials in cardiogenic shock. IABP-SHOCK II remains the foundational trial that overturned two decades of Class I guideline support for IABP in cardiogenic shock — and its lessons are directly relevant to interpreting the emerging mechanical-circulatory-support trial landscape.

Trial design

Randomised, prospective, open-label, multi-centre (37 German centres). N=600 patients (IABP n=301; control n=299). Eligible: acute MI complicated by cardiogenic shock with expected early revascularisation (PCI or CABG). Primary endpoint: 30-day all-cause mortality.

Key results

30-day all-cause mortality: 39.7% (IABP) vs 41.3% (control) — RR 0.96 (95% CI 0.79–1.17; P=0.69). No significant differences in any secondary endpoint: time to haemodynamic stabilisation, catecholamine dose, or lactate clearance. Safety: major bleeding 3.3% vs 4.4% (P=0.51); peripheral ischaemia 4.3% vs 3.4% (P=0.53); sepsis 15.7% vs 20.5% (P=0.15); stroke 0.7% vs 1.7% (P=0.28) — all non-significant. Twelve-month follow-up confirmed sustained absence of survival benefit.

Clinical impact

IABP-SHOCK II directly drove the ESC to downgrade IABP from Class I to Class III (no benefit, possible harm) in the 2012 and subsequent AMI-CS guidelines — one of the fastest and most consequential guideline downgrades after a landmark trial in modern cardiology. The null result reshaped the cardiogenic shock device landscape toward active support strategies (Impella, TandemHeart, VA-ECMO), whose own randomised evidence base is still being established.

Substudies

Sex-difference substudy (PMID 25287767): women (187/600, 31%) were older with a worse baseline risk profile (lower blood pressure, more diabetes and hypertension) and had higher unadjusted mortality on day 1 (P=0.004), but the difference lost statistical significance after multivariable adjustment, with no sex-related differences in outcome at 1, 6, or 12 months. GLP-1 substudy (PMID 38170249): a prognostic biomarker analysis in 172 patients — elevated circulating GLP-1 independently predicted higher all-cause mortality (above vs below median: HR 2.43 [95% CI 1.50–3.94] short-term, 2.46 at 1 year, 2.12 at 6 years; all P<0.001), i.e. a marker of risk, not a therapy. Design-rationale paper (PMID 22709745): describes the trial's prospective, open-label, randomised design and the choice of 30-day all-cause mortality as the primary endpoint.

Lesson for today: A haemodynamically plausible device can fail a rigorous mortality endpoint. The next generation of cardiogenic shock device trials must enrol rapidly, power for mortality, avoid enrichment bias, and resist premature adoption before randomised evidence arrives.
