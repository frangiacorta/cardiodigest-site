Industry & Regulatory

P2Y12 inhibitor monotherapy after PCI — long-term coagulation impact, Phase 3

NCT07582835 (Phase 3) is studying the long-term effects of P2Y12 inhibitor monotherapy versus standard dual antiplatelet therapy on coagulation markers and clinical outcomes after percutaneous coronary angioplasty. As single-antiplatelet strategies have gained traction in high-bleeding-risk and ticagrelor-monotherapy paradigms (TWILIGHT, GLOBAL LEADERS), this trial adds a mechanistic coagulation-biomarker arm to clarify the biological rationale. Evidence informing the optimal P2Y12 monotherapy duration and agent for the broadest PCI population remains incomplete. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

Empagliflozin initiated early in cardiogenic shock — Phase 3 efficacy trial

NCT05879276 (Phase 3) evaluates the effect of early empagliflozin initiation in cardiogenic shock patients on 3-month all-cause mortality, rehospitalisation, LVEF recovery, and renal function. SGLT2 inhibitors improve post-myocardial-infarction outcomes and may have cardio-renal protective effects in the acute-shock phase, but no randomised data exist in this critically ill population. The trial directly tests whether acute SGLT2i initiation is safe and beneficial when the haemodynamic state is most compromised. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

CORTISHOCK-P — corticosteroids in inflammation-enriched HF cardiogenic shock, Phase 2

NCT07461961 (Phase 2) is recruiting patients with heart-failure-related cardiogenic shock enriched for systemic inflammation, randomising to corticosteroids versus placebo. Inflammatory pathways play an underappreciated role in the pathogenesis of non-ischaemic cardiogenic shock, and uncontrolled data suggest a subset of patients may respond to immunomodulatory strategies. This is the first randomised evaluation of steroid therapy in cardiogenic shock stratified by inflammatory biomarker burden. ClinicalTrials.gov ↗

Practice-changing

— Mavacamten in obstructive versus non-obstructive HCM: meta-analysis of 5 RCTs
Alazzam AY et al. · Heart 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Mavacamten, a selective cardiac myosin inhibitor, is approved for obstructive hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (oHCM). Whether its efficacy and safety profile differs meaningfully between obstructive and non-obstructive (nHCM) phenotypes had not been systematically quantified across trials.
Methods
Meta-analysis of 5 randomised controlled trials enrolling a total of 1,083 patients with HCM (obstructive and non-obstructive). Outcomes analysed included NYHA functional class improvement and the risk of LVEF falling below 50%.
Key results
In oHCM, mavacamten was strongly associated with NYHA class improvement (OR 4.94) and carried a moderate risk of LVEF <50% (OR 2.08). In nHCM, mavacamten showed no significant functional benefit while the risk of LVEF <50% was markedly elevated (OR 14.35 vs 2.08 in oHCM), indicating substantially greater susceptibility to myocardial depression in the non-obstructive phenotype.
Conclusion
Mavacamten benefits obstructive HCM with a manageable systolic risk profile; in non-obstructive HCM it offers no demonstrable functional improvement and carries a pronounced risk of LV systolic impairment, supporting strict phenotype-guided use.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Pooled meta-analysis of 5 RCTs. Statistical pooling across trials with different drug doses, baseline LVEF ranges, and outcome definitions is an inherent limitation; however, the direction and magnitude of the nHCM safety signal is consistent.
Clinical relevance
This finding directly informs prescribing. Off-label use of mavacamten in nHCM is occurring and this meta-analysis provides level 1 evidence that it is not only inefficacious but potentially harmful, with a 14-fold increase in the risk of LVEF dropping below 50%. For interventional cardiologists co-managing HCM patients before or after septal ablation, this evidence recalibrates expectation about mavacamten's scope.
Context
EXPLORER-HCM established mavacamten's efficacy in oHCM (NYHA improvement, LVEF safety monitoring introduced). VALOR-HCM showed it could avoid septal reduction therapy in a pre-invasive setting. The current meta-analysis extends the dataset to cover nHCM, filling a gap in prescribing guidance that remains absent from most guidelines.
Limitations
Meta-analyses pool trial-level rather than patient-level data; HCM subtypes may not be perfectly balanced across included trials; the nHCM trials were smaller and duration of follow-up was variable.
Bottom line: Confirm obstructive phenotype before prescribing mavacamten — non-obstructive HCM shows no functional benefit and a 14-fold risk of LVEF <50%, making off-label use in nHCM difficult to justify.
— Sodium Bicarbonate for In-Hospital Cardiac Arrest: A Randomised Clinical Trial
Granfeldt A et al. · JAMA 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Sodium bicarbonate is widely used empirically during in-hospital cardiac arrest to correct metabolic acidosis and theoretically improve cardiac responsiveness to defibrillation, despite an absence of randomised evidence demonstrating benefit.
Methods
Randomised clinical trial at 21 Danish hospitals enrolling 779 adult patients with in-hospital cardiac arrest. Patients were assigned to sodium bicarbonate versus placebo administered during resuscitation. The primary outcome was return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC); secondary outcomes included 30-day survival and neurological function.
Key results
ROSC occurred in 39% of the bicarbonate group versus 37% in the placebo group (RR 1.05), with no significant difference in 30-day neurological outcome. Alkalosis and hypernatraemia were more common in the bicarbonate group.
Conclusion
Routine sodium bicarbonate administration during in-hospital cardiac arrest does not improve ROSC or favourable neurological outcome at 30 days compared with placebo.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Randomised placebo-controlled trial across 21 hospitals. The design is robust; blinding to bicarbonate vs placebo is feasible in a resuscitation context and the multi-hospital sample improves generalisability within the Danish healthcare setting. At n=779 this is among the largest cardiac arrest intervention trials outside of hypothermia.
Clinical relevance
Sodium bicarbonate is a fixture of resuscitation carts worldwide and is reflexively administered during prolonged IHCA by many teams. This trial provides the first large randomised test of this practice and finds it ineffective while introducing unnecessary electrolyte disturbance. Resuscitation protocols and training curricula should incorporate this null result.
Context
Current AHA/ERC guidelines already note lack of evidence for routine bicarbonate in cardiac arrest, suggesting it only in specific circumstances (tricyclic toxicity, severe pre-existing acidosis, hyperkalaemia). This RCT strengthens those equivocal guideline statements into a more definitive negative recommendation for routine use.
Limitations
Single-country (Danish) healthcare system; blinding may be imperfect if team members recognise pH changes on blood gas; the dose and timing of bicarbonate administration may not represent all practices; out-of-hospital cardiac arrest was not studied.
Bottom line: Sodium bicarbonate has no role in routine resuscitation of in-hospital cardiac arrest — remove it from reflexive resuscitation algorithms and reserve for specific metabolic indications as per guidelines.
— The Clinical and Economic Consequences of Delayed Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement
Vemulapalli S et al. · Structural Heart 2026 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis carries a dismal prognosis without intervention, yet delays between diagnosis and TAVR are common due to referral patterns, institutional capacity, and patient-related factors. The magnitude of harm from delay has not been rigorously quantified in a large contemporary cohort.
Methods
Analysis of 4,069 patients who underwent TAVR. Patients who experienced delayed TAVR (procedural delay greater than 90 days from index evaluation) were compared with those who received timely TAVR. Primary endpoint: 3-year all-cause mortality. Secondary: heart failure hospitalisations and healthcare costs over 3 years.
Key results
Delayed TAVR was associated with a 50% higher risk of 3-year all-cause mortality (HR 1.50), a 59% increase in heart failure hospitalisations (HR 1.59), and $36,740 in excess costs over 3 years compared with timely TAVR.
Conclusion
Delays exceeding 90 days from evaluation to TAVR result in substantially worse survival, more heart failure hospitalisations, and significantly higher costs, providing a compelling case for rapid-access structural heart pathways.
Critical appraisal
Study design
Observational cohort study of 4,069 TAVR patients with a predefined delay threshold of 90 days. Observational design risks confounding — sicker or more frail patients may both delay longer and have worse outcomes. Propensity adjustment would help, and the paper's analytical approach should be scrutinised before discounting confounding as an explanation.
Clinical relevance
An HR of 1.50 for mortality with delay exceeding 90 days is clinically large. Even if partially confounded, the direction is biologically plausible and consistent with the natural history of untreated severe AS (annual mortality 25–50% once symptomatic). The economic signal — nearly $37,000 in excess costs from delay — makes this a quality-improvement and payer argument as much as a clinical one. Rapid-access chest pain equivalents for aortic stenosis have been piloted; this analysis justifies broader adoption.
Context
ESC 2021 and ACC/AHA 2021 valve guidelines recommend TAVR without undue delay in symptomatic patients. Real-world data consistently show median time-to-TAVR of 3–6 months; this study quantifies what that gap costs. The European "Heart Teams for Heart Valves" initiative and US TVT registry have both flagged delay as a quality metric.
Limitations
Retrospective observational design; unmeasured confounders (e.g. trajectory of symptoms, comorbidity burden) may partly explain the mortality signal; definition of "delayed" as >90 days is arbitrary; cost estimates are healthcare-system specific.
Bottom line: Every 90-day delay to TAVR adds measurable mortality risk and nearly $37,000 in excess costs — build rapid-access structural heart pathways to close this gap.
ReTAVI — Early Outcomes of Redo-TAVI with the SAPIEN 3 Platform
Tarantini G et al. · EuroIntervention 2026 · source ↗

This prospective international registry enrolled 143 consecutive patients (median age 84 years; 40.6% female; median STS score 7.0%) with failed transcatheter heart valves undergoing redo-TAVI with a balloon-expandable SAPIEN 3 across 59 centres; failing prostheses were predominantly self-expanding CoreValve/Evolut (53.1%), with isolated regurgitation as the most common failure mode (48.6%). Device success was achieved in 95.1%, with 30-day all-cause mortality of 3.5%, stroke rate 0.7%, and pacemaker implantation in 6.3%; mean transvalvular gradient fell by 12.0 mmHg overall and by 29.0 mmHg in stenotic failures, with coronary obstruction occurring in only 1.4% of cases despite the redo context. These results support redo-TAVI with SAPIEN 3 as a reproducible and low-complication strategy across the full spectrum of failed THV types when guided by Heart Team planning and comprehensive CT imaging.

ARISE FLUIDS — Vasopressors or Fluids in Early Septic Shock
ARISE FLUIDS Investigators et al. · N Engl J Med 2026 · source ↗

This RCT randomised 1,000 emergency department patients with early septic shock to early vasopressor administration with restricted initial fluid resuscitation versus a conventional fluid-first strategy with later vasopressors; at 90 days, days alive and out of hospital did not differ between strategies (76 days in both groups). Pulmonary oedema was significantly less common in the vasopressor-early group (0.6% vs 5.0%), suggesting a fluid-sparing benefit without mortality detriment. For interventional cardiologists managing post-PCI septic patients or shock of mixed aetiology, this null result indicates that early vasopressors are a safe alternative to aggressive fluid loading pending haemodynamic clarification.

DECLARE-TIMI 58 — SGLT2 Inhibition in Carriers of Cardiomyopathy-Associated Genetic Variants
Marston NA et al. · Nature Medicine 2026 · source ↗

In a pre-specified exome sequencing substudy of DECLARE-TIMI 58, dapagliflozin reduced hospitalisation for heart failure with a hazard ratio of 0.18 and an absolute risk reduction of 13% among carriers of cardiomyopathy-associated genetic variants — compared with HR 0.70 and ARR 1% in non-carriers — suggesting effect modification by underlying sarcomeric or cytoskeletal gene status. This finding opens a precision-medicine rationale for early SGLT2i initiation in patients found to carry cardiomyopathy variants during genetic testing, a population increasingly identified in structural heart and HCM clinics. The absolute risk reduction magnitude (13%) is among the largest seen with any drug treatment in heart failure subgroup analyses and warrants prospective validation.

GeminiOne — Novel Transcatheter Edge-to-Edge Repair System for Degenerative MR: 1-Year Outcomes
Zhu D et al. · JACC Asia 2026 · source ↗

The GeminiOne TEER device — a groove-cam locking mechanism designed to reduce residual MR — was evaluated in 120 high-surgical-risk patients with degenerative mitral regurgitation, achieving 89.84% clinical success and MR reduction to ≤2+ in 92.24% at 1 year; NYHA functional class I/II improved from 23% at baseline to 95% at 1 year, with 98.3% freedom from reintervention. The entry of a novel TEER platform with distinct locking architecture into the evidence base broadens the device ecosystem for structural MR, particularly in patients with complex valve anatomy challenging for existing clips. Longer-term durability and head-to-head comparison with established TEER systems remain to be established.

— Cusp-Overlap View Technique Reduces Conduction Disturbance and PPI After Self-Expanding TAVR
Salem M et al. · J Clin Med 2026 · source ↗

In 203 patients undergoing self-expanding TAVR, the cusp-overlap technique (COT) versus the conventional three-cusp coplanar view was associated with significantly lower rates of high-grade AV block (13.2% vs 26.8%; P=0.023) and 30-day permanent pacemaker implantation (12.2% vs 24.7%; P=0.021), with COT independently associated with an OR of 0.45 for any conduction disturbance. Conduction injury from self-expanding valves remains a major quality metric and patient-outcome concern; a simple fluoroscopic optimisation that halves PPI rates is an immediately actionable technical improvement. This adds to a growing body of evidence supporting routine COT adoption for self-expanding systems, though operator learning curves and institutional standardisation require attention.

— Risk and Outcomes of Reintervention After Transcatheter Aortic Valve Implantation — Medicare Data
Alabbadi S et al. · Ann Thorac Surg 2026 · source ↗

Among 324,701 Medicare beneficiaries undergoing TAVR, only 0.7% required reintervention, but the annual reintervention hazard rose from 0.26% per year in the early phase (before 3.5 years) to 0.98% per year after 7 years, with valve stenosis accounting for 70.8% of late reinterventions. As the TAVR population extends to younger and lower-risk patients with expected long valve lifespans, late structural valve deterioration and reintervention planning will become central clinical concerns. These data from one of the largest TAVR administrative datasets available provide the most precise estimate of reintervention kinetics yet, supporting a 5–7 year valve surveillance strategy and early consideration of redo planning for first-time TAVR recipients.

— Timing of IABP Initiation and Its Impact on Outcomes in AMI-Cardiogenic Shock
Bojti I et al. · Clin Res Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

In this retrospective bi-centre analysis of 399 AMI-cardiogenic shock patients, early IABP insertion during PCI versus no IABP was associated with significantly lower in-hospital mortality (adjusted OR 0.29), with the greatest benefit observed in patients with moderate myocardial area at risk (30–55%); rescue IABP after haemodynamic deterioration provided a weaker and less consistent benefit. Following the neutral result of IABP-SHOCK II in unselected shock, this analysis suggests that timing and patient selection — rather than device use per se — may explain prior trial results and that early prophylactic IABP during PCI may still benefit carefully selected AMI-CS patients. These findings are hypothesis-generating and warrant prospective validation in a randomised design stratified by timing of insertion.

— Single vs Augmented Antiplatelet Therapy with Anticoagulation in AMI-Cardiogenic Shock Requiring VA-ECMO
Chowdhury JM et al. · Perfusion 2026 · source ↗

In 72 patients with AMI-CS treated with PCI and VA-ECMO, there were no significant differences in in-stent thrombosis between aspirin monotherapy, augmented antiplatelet regimens, or no antiplatelet therapy; intracranial haemorrhage occurred more frequently in the augmented antiplatelet group. The optimal antithrombotic regimen in VA-ECMO patients after PCI is a critical unresolved question: ECMO circuits require anticoagulation, stents require antiplatelet therapy, and the combination creates an additive bleeding risk — aspirin monotherapy may be an adequate and safer middle ground in this highly selected population. These observational data are limited by small sample size and retrospective design but provide an important signal for antiplatelet stewardship in this vulnerable group.

— IVUS-Guided vs Angiography-Guided PCI for Unprotected Left Main — Meta-Analysis of RCTs
Elhakim A et al. · Clin Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

Meta-analysis of 4 randomised trials enrolling 2,278 patients with unprotected left main coronary artery disease found numerically lower mortality, cardiac death, target lesion revascularisation, and target vessel revascularisation with IVUS-guided PCI versus angiography-guided PCI, but none of these differences reached statistical significance. Left main PCI is a high-stakes decision with no margin for suboptimal stenting; the consistent numerical signal in favour of IVUS guidance across multiple endpoints, combined with established Class I guideline recommendations, further supports routine IVUS use — the lack of statistical significance likely reflects insufficient power rather than absence of true effect. Larger powered trials specifically in unprotected left main disease remain needed to definitively confirm or exclude an IVUS benefit at the MACE level.

— Residual Inflammation and Cholesterol Risk on Non-Culprit Plaque Progression: Serial OCT in ACS
Zhang D et al. · BMC Cardiovasc Disord 2026 · source ↗

In 243 ACS patients with serial OCT imaging, residual inflammatory risk (on-treatment hs-CRP ≥2 mg/L) was independently associated with luminal narrowing in non-culprit vessels (OR 2.92), while residual cholesterol risk and combined residual cholesterol-inflammation risk independently predicted persistence of thin-cap fibroatheroma (TCFA) at follow-up. These findings provide OCT-level mechanistic support for dual residual-risk-guided therapy in post-ACS patients — antiplatelet and statin optimisation alone may be insufficient where subclinical inflammation persists. The combination risk-stratification approach (RCR/RCIR) could guide future trials of anti-inflammatory agents (colchicine, IL-6 inhibition) in the highest-risk non-culprit plaque subgroup.

— Improving the Diagnostic Performance of High-Sensitivity Troponin for AMI in Renal Impairment
Collard D et al. · Heart 2026 · source ↗

In an analysis of 221,175 patients, the standard hs-TnT 14 ng/L cutoff produced false-positive rates of 68–93% for AMI diagnosis in patients with eGFR 15–60 mL/min; eGFR-specific cutoffs (73 ng/L for eGFR 45–60, 112 ng/L for eGFR 30–44, and 184 ng/L for eGFR 15–29 mL/min) maintained approximately 70% AMI detection sensitivity while reducing false-positive rates to approximately 15%. CKD patients admitted after PCI or presenting with chest pain represent a common diagnostic dilemma given constitutively elevated troponin; eGFR-adjusted thresholds offer a practical and analytically validated strategy to reduce unnecessary investigation and improve rule-in accuracy. Implementation requires laboratory reporting systems to flag CKD-adjusted interpretations alongside the standard result.

— Radial vs Femoral Access for Coronary Angiography in Critically Ill ICU Patients
Devaux A et al. · J Anesth Analg Crit Care 2026 · source ↗

In 235 ICU patients requiring coronary angiography (61.7% post-cardiac arrest, 49.4% in cardiogenic shock), femoral versus radial access was associated with a significantly worse composite outcome (win ratio 0.62, P=0.04), driven primarily by increased access-site complications, with no mortality difference between groups. Radial access has become standard in elective PCI; this ICU-specific cohort demonstrates that its benefits extend to the critically ill population, where femoral access has traditionally been preferred for haemodynamic support device compatibility. Operators should maintain radial-first reflexes even in shock and cardiac arrest when haemodynamic support is not simultaneously required through femoral access.

— ATTR-CA Prevalence and Risk-Score Performance in Severe Aortic Stenosis Before TAVI
Katarzyna G et al. · Front Cardiovasc Med 2026 · source ↗

In a prospective cohort of 104 patients with severe AS undergoing evaluation for TAVI, transthyretin amyloid cardiomyopathy (ATTR-CA) was identified in 18% — a markedly higher prevalence than in the general population — with ATTR-CA patients more commonly presenting with low-flow/low-gradient AS, higher NT-proBNP and troponin, and the eRAISE score achieving an AUC of 0.948 for diagnosis. The co-existence of ATTR-CA and AS is increasingly recognised as a dual-pathology condition that alters prognosis and may require concurrent tafamidis therapy in addition to TAVI; routine screening in all TAVI candidates using non-invasive tools (⁹⁹mTc scintigraphy or CMR) and risk scores is emerging as best practice. These data support integration of ATTR-CA screening into pre-TAVI protocols at structural heart centres.

— Antidotes for Anticoagulation Reversal — State of the Art
Rocca B et al. · N Engl J Med 2026 · source ↗

This comprehensive NEJM review covers reversal strategies across all anticoagulant classes, highlighting idarucizumab as the specific antidote for dabigatran, 4F-PCC for VKA and factor Xa inhibitor reversal, and reviewing the ongoing debate between 4F-PCC and andexanet alfa for apixaban/rivaroxaban reversal, including emerging evidence and cost-effectiveness considerations. Interventional cardiologists routinely manage patients on dual anticoagulant and antiplatelet therapy who require urgent or emergent catheterisation; the practical guidance in this review directly informs peri-procedural anticoagulation reversal decisions. The review also addresses emerging agents in development and the importance of institutional protocols given the rapidly expanding reversal armamentarium.

Tier 3

Worth knowing

— Moderate AS with Concomitant Mitral Regurgitation: A High-Risk Phenotype
Gabr EM et al. · JACC Cardiovasc Imaging 2026 · source ↗

In 742 patients with at least moderate AS on CMR, the 76 patients with combined moderate AS and moderate-or-greater MR showed 80.3% in NYHA class II–IV at presentation and an adjusted HR of 1.86 (95% CI 1.31–2.65) for cardiovascular death or HF hospitalisation over a median 1.8 years compared with isolated moderate AS — an outcome burden comparable to severe AS populations meeting guideline intervention thresholds. This dual-valvular phenotype represents a therapeutic gap: neither lesion individually meets standard intervention criteria yet together they confer severe-AS-equivalent risk, particularly in the low-flow subgroup (stroke volume index ≤35 mL/m²). CMR-based quantification of the dual burden offers superior risk stratification and may support earlier intervention discussions in this underserved population.

— SGLT2 Inhibitors After TAVR: Real-World Propensity-Matched Analysis
Zahid S et al. · Structural Heart 2026 · source ↗

In a propensity-matched analysis of 61,763 TAVR patients (3,529 on SGLT2 inhibitors), SGLT2i use post-TAVR was associated with significantly lower 3-year all-cause mortality (HR 0.70), fewer HF exacerbations, and no increase in acute kidney injury or genitourinary infections. While a randomised trial in the TAVR population has not been completed (DAPA-TAVI is ongoing), these real-world data strengthen the biological and clinical rationale for proactive SGLT2i initiation in TAVR recipients who have concurrent diabetes, CKD, or reduced LVEF. Structural heart teams should systematically review GDMT gaps at the post-TAVR follow-up visit.

— Sex-Related Differences in Outcomes of Patients with Calcified Coronary Artery Disease Undergoing PCI
Lin Z et al. · Catheter Cardiovasc Interv 2026 · source ↗

Among 10,681 consecutive PCI patients in the prospective Fuwai PCI Registry, moderate-to-severe coronary artery calcification (MSCAC) was present in 18.24% overall, with higher prevalence in women over 62 years (adjusted OR 1.24, 95% CI 1.01–1.52); among MSCAC patients, women faced a markedly higher risk of MACE compared with men (adjusted HR 2.89, 95% CI 1.56–5.34), driven primarily by cardiovascular death and stroke. This large registry establishes sex and age as effect modifiers of calcification-associated PCI risk, suggesting that lesion preparation strategies (IVL, rotational atherectomy) may need to be applied with heightened urgency in post-menopausal women with heavily calcified lesions. Prospective studies specifically designed to evaluate calcification management strategies in women are lacking and urgently needed.

COMPARE-TAVI 1 — CMR Reclassification of Aortic Regurgitation After TAVI
Pedersen AAU et al. · J Cardiovasc Magn Reson 2026 · source ↗

In 327 patients from the COMPARE-TAVI 1 substudy, TTE and CMR agreed on AR grade in 86% of post-TAVI patients; CMR downgraded all 5 patients classified as moderate or greater AR by TTE, while the single patient with moderate AR confirmed by CMR was not identified by TTE. These findings suggest that TTE both overcalls and undercalls clinically significant AR post-TAVI, reinforcing CMR as the reference standard for post-TAVI AR surveillance, particularly when clinical decisions about anticoagulation or reintervention hinge on AR quantification. Routine CMR at post-TAVI follow-up is logistically demanding but may be warranted when TTE findings are equivocal or when AR has potential treatment implications.

— Safety and Outcomes of Concomitant LAA Occlusion and AF Ablation
Sawalha K et al. · JACC Advances 2026 · source ↗

In a propensity-matched TriNetX cohort of 1,899 patients in each group, concomitant AF ablation at the time of Watchman LAAO was not associated with increased 90-day mortality (0.6% vs 0.6%; HR 1.00, 95% CI 0.43–2.32), 1-year mortality (1.4% vs 1.5%; HR 0.97, 95% CI 0.58–1.63), stroke, device leak, device-related thrombus, or pericardial effusion compared with LAAO alone. The combined single-procedure approach offers efficiency for patients who are candidates for both rhythm control and stroke prevention; this large matched cohort provides the most reassuring safety signal to date for the combined strategy. Randomised confirmation is still lacking, and patient selection — particularly regarding AF type, anatomy, and ablation technique — requires individualised assessment.

— IVUS-Guided vs Angiography-Guided Intracoronary Brachytherapy for In-Stent Restenosis
Kharsa C et al. · JACC Advances 2026 · source ↗

In 221 patients undergoing intracoronary brachytherapy for in-stent restenosis (132 IVUS-guided), IVUS use was associated with larger measured vessel diameters, more frequent lesion preparation, and longer radiation dwell times; nominal rates of all-cause mortality (8.3% vs 23.6%; P=0.03) and periprocedural MI (11.4% vs 25.8%; P=0.005) were lower in the IVUS group, but on Cox regression IVUS guidance was not independently associated with improved long-term outcomes and target lesion revascularisation was actually higher in the IVUS group (HR 1.68, 95% CI 1.05–2.68). The paradox — better nominal outcomes but not independent benefit after adjustment — likely reflects selection bias: operators using IVUS tended to treat more complex lesions that, despite improved preparation, have higher baseline recurrence risk. IVUS should still be used for brachytherapy planning, but these results caution against assuming it independently improves clinical outcomes.

— Palliative and End-of-Life Care Utilisation After AMI-Cardiogenic Shock
Sterling LH et al. · JACC Advances 2026 · source ↗

Among 3,881 AMI-cardiogenic shock survivors who subsequently died in a population-based Ontario cohort (median post-discharge survival 1,096 days), 64% received palliative care in the final year of life, but initiation was most commonly in the last 14 days (47.7%); earlier palliative care referral was independently associated with reduced odds of dying in hospital (adjusted OR 0.50) and in the ICU (adjusted OR 0.34). The late initiation pattern reflects structural barriers to early goals-of-care discussions in cardiology — AMI-CS survivors have a high 1-year mortality that rivals many terminal oncological diagnoses, yet palliative integration is systematically delayed. Proactive palliative consultation and goals-of-care planning should be embedded in standard post-shock follow-up protocols.

MIRON-PREDICT — Explainable AI Predicts Intramyocardial Haemorrhage Before Reperfusion in STEMI
Youssef K et al. · JACC Advances 2026 · source ↗

A superposable neural network trained on 252 STEMI patients from MIRON-PREDICT identified three cath-lab-available variables — coronary collateral presence, degree of coronary artery occlusion, and sum ST-score — to predict intramyocardial haemorrhage (IMH) before revascularisation with 84.9% accuracy, 82.3% sensitivity, and 87.3% specificity, validated in a 36-patient holdout cohort (83.3% accuracy). IMH complicates approximately 40% of reperfused STEMIs and is strongly associated with adverse remodelling and outcomes; pre-reperfusion identification could enable selective application of IMH-mitigating strategies (intracoronary sodium nitroprusside, glycoprotein inhibitors, pre-revascularisation anti-oedema therapy). This practically deployable bedside score represents a meaningful step toward personalised STEMI care, pending larger validation.

— Iliofemoral Access-Site Calcification Volume Predicts Long-Term Mortality After TAVI
Vij V et al. · Clin Res Cardiol 2026 · source ↗

In 1,067 TAVI patients, high CT-derived iliofemoral calcification volume was independently associated with markedly higher 3-year mortality (34.3% vs 22.3%) and 5-year mortality (42.2% vs 28.7%) without increasing peri-procedural complication rates, reflecting systemic atherosclerotic disease burden rather than a specific procedural risk. Iliofemoral calcification is routinely assessed for access-site planning but is not commonly incorporated into long-term prognostic models; its independent mortality signal likely captures diffuse atherosclerosis that predicts adverse outcomes through non-procedural mechanisms. This finding supports integrating iliofemoral calcification scoring into TAVI risk models and may influence post-procedural secondary prevention intensity.

— Statin Initiation Associated with Lower Incident Frailty in Older Veterans
Qazi S et al. · Eur Heart J 2026 · source ↗

In 987,301 statin-naïve US veterans aged ≥67 years with follow-up of 5.3 years, new statin initiators showed a 24% lower risk of incident frailty or death (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.75–0.76) versus non-initiators after overlap propensity score weighting, with consistent results in pre-frail patients. Frailty is a growing concern in PCI and TAVR patient selection and an independent predictor of procedural risk — statins' anti-inflammatory pleiotropic effects may contribute to frailty prevention beyond LDL lowering. This observational signal is noteworthy given the magnitude and consistency but requires cautious interpretation given the limitations of administrative data and residual confounding.

— Valve-in-Valve TAVR vs Redo Surgery in Women with Degenerated Aortic Bioprosthesis
Tagliafierro M et al. · Catheter Cardiovasc Interv 2026 · source ↗

In 132 women with degenerated surgical aortic bioprostheses undergoing ViV TAVR (n=92) or redo-SAVR (n=40), 30-day mortality was comparable (1.1% vs 2.5%, P=0.303), as were 1-year MACE (12.0% vs 12.5%), pacemaker (3.3% vs 2.5%), stroke (4.3% vs 5.0%), and rehospitalisation rates before and after propensity matching. Women historically face higher reintervention risks than men; this dedicated female cohort demonstrates that both ViV and redo-SAVR are feasible options with equivalent short-term outcomes, supporting a Heart Team discussion centred on anatomy, prosthesis size, and patient preference rather than sex-specific contraindications to either approach. Longer follow-up is needed to assess valve durability outcomes by strategy.

LOQI — Extended Duration Impella 5.5 Support Is Safe: Analysis from the LOQI Registry
Kaczorowski D et al. · JHLT Open 2026 · source ↗

In the LOQI registry of 443 patients supported with Impella 5.5, extended support duration exceeding 14 days did not increase event-adjusted serious adverse event rates compared with shorter support durations (SAE rate 0.0235 vs 0.0776 events per day), suggesting that device-related complications do not accumulate linearly with longer support times. Prolonged MCS as a bridge to recovery or transplant decision is increasingly used for refractory cardiogenic shock; this registry provides reassurance that extended Impella 5.5 support is logistically feasible without a disproportionate safety penalty. These data support careful patient selection for extended mechanical unloading strategies, while acknowledging that registry data preclude causal inference about long-term device-related events.

Landmark Revisited

DanGer Shock — Microaxial Flow Pump or Standard Care in Infarct-Related Cardiogenic Shock
Møller JE et al. · N Engl J Med 2024;390:1382–1393 · primary source ↗
Structured abstract
Background
Despite advances in primary PCI, STEMI complicated by cardiogenic shock (AMICS) carries 30–50% in-hospital mortality. Temporary mechanical circulatory support with a microaxial flow pump (Impella CP) had become widespread in many European and US centres without prospective randomised evidence of mortality benefit; prior trials (IABP-SHOCK II) had failed to show benefit with older-generation devices.
Methods
International, multicenter, open-label randomised trial at 14 tertiary centres across Denmark, Germany, and the UK. Patients with STEMI and cardiogenic shock were randomised 1:1 to Impella CP plus standard care or standard care alone. Randomisation occurred before primary PCI where possible. The primary endpoint was all-cause mortality at 180 days. A pre-specified composite safety endpoint comprised severe bleeding, limb ischaemia, haemolysis, device failure, or worsening aortic regurgitation. NCT01633502.
Key results
Of 360 randomised patients, 355 were included in the final analysis (179 Impella CP group, 176 standard-care group). Median age was 67 years; 79.2% were men. All-cause mortality at 180 days was 45.8% in the Impella CP group versus 58.5% in the standard-care group (HR 0.74; 95% CI 0.55–0.99; P=0.04). The composite safety endpoint occurred in 24.0% of the Impella CP group versus 6.2% of the standard-care group (RR 4.74; 95% CI 2.36–9.55). Renal replacement therapy was required in 41.9% of Impella CP patients versus 26.7% of standard-care patients (RR 1.98; 95% CI 1.27–3.09).
Conclusion
Routine use of a microaxial flow pump reduced 180-day all-cause mortality by an absolute 12.7 percentage points in STEMI-related cardiogenic shock compared with standard care alone, at the cost of a substantially higher rate of device-related adverse events and renal replacement therapy requirement.
The question & the verdict

DanGer Shock asked the question that the field had been debating for nearly two decades: does active haemodynamic support with a percutaneous microaxial flow pump, inserted early in the course of STEMI-related cardiogenic shock, save lives? The context was one of widespread empirical Impella use in high-volume centres without supporting RCT evidence — a practice driven by biological plausibility, registry signals, and expert extrapolation from single-centre experience. The trial answered affirmatively: a hazard ratio of 0.74 for 180-day mortality represents a meaningful, statistically significant benefit, and the 12.7 absolute percentage point reduction translates to a number needed to treat of approximately 8 patients. Uniquely among MCS trials, DanGer Shock enrolled only STEMI patients (excluding non-STEMI, out-of-hospital cardiac arrest primary, and mixed shock aetiologies) and mandated pre-PCI Impella insertion where feasible — a cleaner population and earlier timing than prior trials. The verdict is positive but contextually important: the benefit is real but does not come free of risk, and patient selection matters profoundly.

Statistical appraisal

The trial used an intention-to-treat analysis with a primary endpoint at 180 days, powered to detect an approximately 12–15 percentage point absolute mortality reduction. The hazard ratio of 0.74 with a 95% CI of 0.55–0.99 means the upper confidence limit just crosses the 1.0 threshold — a nominally significant but relatively narrow margin of statistical certainty. This CI width reflects the sample size (n=355 analysable) and the inherent heterogeneity of cardiogenic shock populations, even when restricted to STEMI. The composite safety signal (RR 4.74 for adverse events) and renal replacement therapy signal (RR 1.98) are compelling and must be weighed against the mortality benefit — these were not competing risks but additive harms. The open-label design introduces potential performance bias: centres with Impella familiarity may have delivered superior standard care in the Impella arm, compounding device benefit. Landmark analysis and subgroup data — particularly the age-interaction substudy (PMID 39551167, JACC 2025) showing benefit concentrated in patients under 77 years (OR 0.45; 95% CI 0.28–0.73; P=0.001) with no signal in those ≥77 years (OR 1.52; P for interaction = 0.028) — are critical for interpreting the primary result and identifying who benefits most.

Subsequent substudies & follow-ups

Age-interaction analysis (Klein A et al., JACC 2025; PMID 39551167 ↗): Secondary analysis of DanGer Shock showing that patients <77 years experienced a significant reduction in 180-day mortality with Impella (OR 0.45; 95% CI 0.28–0.73; P=0.001), whereas patients ≥77 years showed no benefit and a non-significant trend to harm (OR 1.52; 95% CI 0.57–4.08; P for interaction = 0.028). Age 77 years emerged as an approximate threshold above which routine mAFP insertion may not be warranted, suggesting the overall trial benefit is driven by younger patients. This substudy directly informs patient selection in clinical practice.

Why it still matters: DanGer Shock is the first RCT to demonstrate a survival benefit from routine Impella use in STEMI-cardiogenic shock — but the benefit is age-dependent, comes with a 4-fold higher device-adverse-event rate, and nearly 2-fold higher renal replacement therapy requirement, demanding careful patient selection rather than universal adoption and validating the field's move toward phenotype-guided mechanical circulatory support.
